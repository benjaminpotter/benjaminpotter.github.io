I think spiritwear is both practical and good looking when used effectively. It has a very distinct purpose: clearly identify the wearer as a member of the requisite community. Often, it has large logos, bright colours, and important phrases. All of these elements combine to ensure the wearer is recognized. I argue that today, the concept of true spiritwear has been eroded into *fashionable* spiritwear. We have forgotten what spirited clothing is really about and in turn, our communities suffer.

### What is *fashionable* spirit wear?
A compromise. Take for example this wildly popular Queen's University sweater.

![A man poses in a green sweater that reads Queen's](/green_sweater.png 'Fashionable Spiritwear') 

It doesn't even contain red, yellow, or blue, the colours associated with Queen's. This is one of the core tenants of good spiritwear.

Typically, the wearer is proud to be part of the community, and they show this by putting on branded spiritwear. Pride and collectivism are, and should be, the driving force behind expression of spirit.
